The Basics

Autism Is a Spectrum

Autism is known as a spectrum condition, which means it affects individuals in many different ways. Every person with autism has a unique set of strengths, challenges, and characteristics, so no two experiences with autism are exactly the same.

Signs often begin to appear in early childhood, typically before the age of three, and the level of support a person may need can range from minimal to more significant. Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a lifelong neurodevelopmental condition that affects how people interact with others, learn, communicate, and behave.

If you notice developmental differences in your child, seeking early support and intervention can make a meaningful difference. Early services can help children develop important communication, social, and learning skills during critical stages of growth.

What to Look For

Early Signs and Characteristics

Autism can show up in three main areas. Understanding these can help families know when to ask for a developmental screening.

Communication

  • Delayed development of spoken language
  • Repeating words or phrases
  • Difficulty using language in socially appropriate ways
  • Trouble starting or continuing conversations
  • Challenges expressing needs, wants, or feelings clearly

Social Interaction

  • Difficulty interpreting verbal and non-verbal communication
  • Limited interest in forming peer relationships
  • Reduced back-and-forth emotional or social interaction
  • Discomfort with physical touch such as hugging
  • Trouble recognizing or discussing emotions
  • Rarely sharing interests or achievements with others

Behavioral Patterns

  • Repetitive behaviors or strong preferences for routines
  • Differences in motor coordination
  • Strong attachment to specific objects
  • Distress when routines or environments change
  • Arranging or lining up toys or objects in specific ways
  • Repetitive movements such as rocking

These signs do not necessarily mean a child has autism, but they may indicate that a developmental screening could be helpful. If you have concerns, speaking with a healthcare professional or early intervention provider can help guide the next steps.

Why It Matters

Early Intervention Is Critical

Following an autism diagnosis, children should be referred for special services. Some children may begin receiving these services even before a formal diagnosis is made.

Generally, children who start special services earlier tend to experience better outcomes. The Lewis County Autism Coalition's SMART program is designed specifically to help families navigate the diagnostic process and connect with services without delay.

Worried About Your Child's Development?

You are not alone. The SMART program is here to help families in Lewis County navigate screening, diagnosis, and next steps with care.
